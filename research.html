Problem Statement

Glasgow University students face challenges in accessing centralized and comprehensive mental health resources. Despite various sources available, the information is scattered and not easily accessible. Additionally, the university's extended waiting times for counseling services, averaging 14 days, exacerbate the need for immediate mental health support. Existing interventions, as highlighted in a 2019 review, demonstrate varying levels of effectiveness, with only 47% considered fully effective. To address these issues, the project, WebWellness, aims to create a dedicated online resource for Glasgow University students, providing preventative measures and tangible interactive exercises to enhance mental health and wellbeing. The goal is to bridge the gap in accessible and effective mental health support for students.

Summary

The primary goal of this topic is to investigate how the web can be used to improve access to mental health resources, particularly for Glasgow University students. It delves into the role of technology, including artificial intelligence advancements, in improving mental health. The proposed website seeks to clarify common myths and misconceptions about mental health, shedding light on these issues. It aims to emphasise the importance of stress management and maintaining good mental health, as well as providing guidance on how to achieve them. Finally, the topic touches on online self-help strategies, particularly interactive exercises like grounding techniques that empower students to take control of their mental well-being through online resources and support.

Reflection Point

My initial topic of interest was Affective computing, but over the course of the first two weeks, I decided to narrow my focus. This shift occurred as I increasingly recognized the significance of user-centered design and integrated feedback. My research into the process led me to explore existing web pages and resources, particularly those linked to the NHS, revealing a lack of centralized sources. Originally, my intention was to create a resource mainly for medical students or individuals interested in psychology and healthcare, with a focus on the role of technology in promoting mental wellbeing. However, I decided to shift towards creating a mental health and well-being website. This decision was driven by my perception of its higher value, especially considering the significant challenges related to mental health access at Glasgow University.
